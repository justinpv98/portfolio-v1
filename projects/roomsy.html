Roomsy

Roomsy is a vacation rental booking service akin to Airbnb. It's a simple landing page with a complex and responsive CSS layout that displays my skill in turning a design from a Figma file to a website.

Process

The intention of this design was simply to expand further on my CSS skills. I found an interesting design on Dribbble and wanted to replicate it. While the original design was elaborate and covered a multi-page layout scheme, I wanted to focus mainly on improving front-end design without being pushed to creating a back-end for it. The landing page was fairly complex as well and would make for good practice.

The designing of the website was fairly simple. I followed the process of designing a website mobile-first. The mobile design for the website was far simpler than the desktop design. The complex part about it was developing a slider purely via CSS. By tackling overflow and using the power of flexbox along with negative margins, it becomes fairly easy.

Making the website desktop-friendly was the next concern, and was considerably more complex than its mobile counterpart. The first slider had to be converted into a grid, and I had to make extensive use of display: grid. The second slider had to be converted into two absolutely positioned blocks of images. Finally, the container for the host call-to-action had to be rounded off which I tackled by using two pseudoelements in the shape of background-matching semicircles.

Lessons Learned

Things that I could say I've learned from doing the Roomsy project:

  1. Complex layouts don't need to be difficult layouts.

    What this means is just because something seems complex in how you would tackle it, doesn't mean it has to be intimidating. The method I used to tackle the design of this project -- particularly in the call-to-action section was thinking how could I simplify the problem as much as I could.

    I had already accomplished making a slider for the category section, and I knew it mostly just consisted of flex items. I also knew that the absolutely positioned cards would technically be in two different containers. So, I solved my problem by simplying adding a wrapper over the containers and setting it to flex, making the containers themselves flex, and making the scroll snap to the individual cards.

    All this required was simply thinking about the problem holistically and then separating it into parts to tackle it.

  2. Improving my ability to replicate designs

    While I can by no means say that my design is pixel-perfect, after all the age of pixel-perfect design in a world of responsitivty is nigh impossible, I would say that I have gained a better ability to transfer a design from image or Figma file over to code.

    A lot of the things we learn through life are through imitation. The idea of social learning for example is a big theory in how we learn psychologically. This also extends to design and UX. I feel like tackling this project in particular made me more cognizant of design and UX considerations when it comes to color and layout.

Conclusion

Spending time to flesh out the Roomsy landing page has definitely helped me become more skilled when it comes to real-world CSS layouts that I would be asked to create and replicate from a design. It also taught me how to approach CSS problems much like one would with problems in a programming language. Think about the problem holistically, break it into the steps, and go from there.
